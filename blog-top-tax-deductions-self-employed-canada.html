Top 15 Tax Deductions for Self-Employed Canadians in 2025

Self-employed Canadians — freelancers, contractors, consultants, and gig workers — have access to a wide range of tax deductions that salaried employees cannot claim. These deductions are reported on Form T2125 and can dramatically reduce your taxable income.

1. Home Office Expenses

If you use a dedicated space regularly and exclusively for business, you can deduct a proportionate share of rent or mortgage interest, property taxes, utilities, home insurance, and maintenance, based on the percentage of your home used for business.

The CRA also offers a simplified flat-rate method ($2/day up to $500/year), but the detailed method usually yields a larger deduction.

2. Vehicle Expenses

If you use your vehicle for business, you can deduct the business-use portion of gas, insurance, maintenance, lease payments or CCA, parking, and registration. You must keep a logbook tracking business vs. personal kilometres.

3–7. Professional Services, Supplies & Equipment

Accounting and legal fees, office supplies, computer equipment, software subscriptions, and internet costs are all deductible. Capital purchases over $500 are claimed via CCA, though immediate expensing may allow full first-year deduction.

Professional development, courses, and certifications related to your business are also deductible.

8–12. Insurance, Meals, Travel & Marketing

Business insurance premiums are fully deductible. Client meals and entertainment are 50% deductible. Business travel is fully deductible when primarily for business. Marketing costs — website hosting, advertising, business cards, and social ads — are fully deductible.

13–15. CPP, Health Spending & Bad Debts

Self-employed Canadians pay both employee and employer CPP portions; the employer portion is deductible. Health Spending Account premiums through your corporation are deductible. And if a client doesn't pay, the bad debt can be written off against income.
