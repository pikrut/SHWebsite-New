How to Incorporate Your Business in Ontario: Step-by-Step Guide

Incorporating a business in Ontario is one of the most impactful financial decisions an entrepreneur can make. A corporation provides limited liability protection, access to the small business tax rate (about 12.2% in Ontario), income-splitting opportunities, and enhanced credibility. This guide walks you through every step.

Federal vs. Provincial Incorporation

You can incorporate federally through Corporations Canada or provincially through the Ontario Business Registry. Federal incorporation gives you name rights across all provinces but requires extra-provincial registration where you operate. Provincial incorporation is simpler and less expensive but limits name protection to Ontario.

For most small businesses operating primarily in Ontario, provincial incorporation is sufficient and more cost-effective.

Step 1: NUANS Name Search

For a named corporation you must first conduct a NUANS report to ensure your proposed name doesn't conflict with existing names or trademarks. A NUANS report costs about $13.80 and is valid for 90 days. A numbered company skips this step.

Step 2: File Articles of Incorporation

Articles are filed with the Ontario Business Registry (provincial) or Corporations Canada (federal). You specify the corporation's name, registered office, share structure, transfer restrictions, and the first directors.

The filing fee is $300 for Ontario provincial incorporation online; federal incorporation is $200 online.

Step 3: Register with the CRA

After incorporation you need a Business Number (BN). You'll also register program accounts: corporate income tax (RC), payroll (RP) if you have employees, GST/HST (RT) if applicable, and import/export (RM) if needed.

Ongoing Compliance Requirements

After incorporation you must file a T2 within six months of year-end, file an annual return with the Ontario Business Registry, maintain a corporate minute book, hold annual shareholder meetings, and keep proper records. Failing to file the annual return can result in dissolution.
