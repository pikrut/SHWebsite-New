Northern Residents Deduction: Complete Guide for Remote Canadian Communities

The Northern Residents Deduction (NRD) is one of the most valuable — and most overlooked — tax benefits for Canadians living in northern and remote communities. If you live in a prescribed zone for at least six consecutive months, you may be entitled to deductions worth hundreds or thousands of dollars. This is especially relevant for our clients in Thunder Bay, Timmins, Sudbury, Sault Ste. Marie, Corner Brook, and Grande Prairie.

Who Qualifies?

To claim the NRD, you must have lived in a prescribed northern or intermediate zone for a continuous period of at least six months. Northern zones include most of Northern Ontario, much of Newfoundland and Labrador, northern Alberta, and the territories. Intermediate zones cover areas like Sudbury and Sault Ste. Marie.

The deduction has two components: a residency amount and a travel benefit amount.

The Residency Amount

For 2024 returns, the basic residency amount is $11.00 per day for northern zone residents and $5.50 per day for intermediate zone residents — roughly $4,015 and $2,008 per year respectively. If you maintain a dwelling and are the only person in the household claiming the deduction, you can claim an additional residency amount, effectively doubling it.

The Travel Benefit

If you receive taxable travel benefits from your employer, you may claim a deduction for up to two trips per household member for personal travel. The CRA uses the lowest return airfare from the nearest designated city as a benchmark.

For Thunder Bay residents the nearest designated city is typically Toronto or Winnipeg; for Corner Brook it would be Halifax or St. John's.

How to Claim on Your T1

The NRD is claimed on Line 25500 using Form T2222. You'll verify your address falls within a prescribed zone, calculate residency days, and determine your travel benefit. Our team is experienced with NRD claims for clients across all our service areas.
